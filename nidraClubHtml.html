Remarks

Total Sleep Time (TST) refers to the actual duration a person spends asleep, excluding wakefulness during the night, and is a critical factor in overall sleep quality and health [1]. Higher TST indicates better sleep efficiency, which is essential for cognitive function, emotional regulation, and reducing the risk of chronic conditions like cardiovascular disease and diabetes [2].

Sleep efficiency is the percentage of time spent asleep relative to the total time spent in bed, with higher values indicating more consolidated and restorative sleep [1]. Poor sleep efficiency, often linked to insomnia and sleep disorders.

Remarks

Understanding sleep stages is essential for assessing overall sleep quality and health [3-4]. Each stage plays a unique role in the restorative process:

• Awake: Normal wakefulness during the night is expected; however, these periods should remain minimal to ensure restorative sleep.

• REM Sleep: REM (Rapid Eye Movement) sleep is critical for dreaming and memory consolidation. The brain’s activity during this phase resembles that of wakefulness, which helps with learning and emotional regulation.

• N1 (Light Sleep): This stage represents the transition from wakefulness to sleep, helping the body to relax initially.

• N2 (Intermediate Sleep): In this phase, the body relaxes more deeply. Heart rate slows, body temperature drops, and it becomes more challenging to awaken—factors that contribute to maintaining sleep continuity.

• N3 (Deep Sleep / Slow-Wave Sleep): As the most restorative stage, deep sleep is vital for physical recovery, growth, and immune function. During N3, the body repairs tissues, builds bone and muscle, and strengthens the immune system, ensuring you wake up feeling refreshed and energetic.

Remarks

Resting Heart Rate (RHR) is the number of heartbeats per minute (bpm) while at rest. A normal RHR for adults is 60–100 bpm, though athletes often have values below 60 bpm due to improved cardiac efficiency [3].

Tachycardia (>100 bpm) An RHR above 100 bpm may indicate dehydration, fever, anxiety, hyperthyroidism, anemia, or cardiac arrhythmias such as atrial fibrillation (AF) or ventricular tachycardia (VT) [4]. In COPD patients, persistent tachycardia can signal an exacerbation.

Bradycardia ('<'60 bpm) An RHR below 60 bpm can be linked to sick sinus syndrome (SSS), heart block (HB), atrioventricular nodal block (AVNB), or medication effects (e.g., beta-blockers) [5]. Severe cases may indicate shock states or CNS depression.

Breathing Rate Trend

Remarks

Resting Breathing Rate (RBR) refers to the number of breaths per minute (brpm) while at rest, without physical exertion or stress. A normal RBR for adults ranges from 12 to 20 brpm, though factors such as age, sex, weight, and overall health can influence respiratory rates [6].

Tachypnea (>20 brpm) An RBR above 20 brpm (tachypnea) may indicate acute illness, cardiovascular or pulmonary disorders, fever, anxiety, or metabolic acidosis (e.g., diabetic ketoacidosis) [7]. In COPD patients, tachypnea, especially when accompanied by shortness of breath, can signal an exacerbation requiring medical attention [8].

Bradypnea (<"12 brpm) An RBR below 12 brpm (bradypnea) can be associated with neurological impairment, metabolic disorders, respiratory depression from opioids or sedatives, sleep apnea, or underlying cardiac or pulmonary disease [7]. In COPD patients, severe bradypnea may indicate declining lung function and a high risk of respiratory failure [8].
